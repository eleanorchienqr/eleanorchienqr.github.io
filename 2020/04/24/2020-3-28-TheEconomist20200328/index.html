Mixed medicine

Congress is poised to pass an emergency stimulus. It is imperfect but necessary.

No textbook was ever written to tell economists what to do in the face of the extraordinary economic hiatus caused by covid-19. Yet as they consider the deserted malls, abandoned offices and billions of lives on hold, the overarching responsibilities of economic policymakers are clear. First, they must protect the incomes of those who cannot work during lockdowns through no fault of their own. Second, they must make sure that growth bounces back fast when daily life resumes.

    • transitive verb. (hold, carry) 平稳握持. she poised the racket above her head; 她把球拍握稳举过头顶
      • (ready) to be poised to do sth/for sth; 为做某事/为某事作好准备
    • noun. uncountable
      • (composure, dignity) 镇定. her admirable poise and assurance 她令人钦佩的沉着与自信
      • (graceful bearing) 仪态. the poise and grace of the dancers/models 舞蹈演员/模特儿的优雅姿态
  • hiatus |BrE: hʌɪˈeɪtəs, AmE: haɪˈeɪdəs | noun. hiatuses or hiatus (pl.) formal (break) 间断
    • economic hiatus 经济间断
  • overarching |BrE: ˌovəˈɑrtʃɪŋ, AmE: ˌəʊvərˈɑ:tʃɪŋ | adj. [only before noun] including or influencing every part of something 包罗万象的; 支配一切的
    • The crisis gave an overarching justification to the government’s policy. 这场危机使政府的政策找到了能解释一切的正当理由。
  • consider … on hold adverb phrase. 让…等待
  • bounce back intransitive verb. 重整旗鼓

The enormous emergency spending bill due to be passed by America’s Congress—which will cost the taxpayer about $2trn (10% of GDP) up front and support much more in new lending —goes some way to discharging both duties. It beefs up unemployment benefits, provides emergency loans and grants to small businesses, and gives $1,200 unconditionally to most Americans. When combined with the ultra-loose monetary policy of the Federal Reserve, which this week announced that it would buy government and mortgage debt in unlimited quantities, the fiscal boost will underpin growth once lockdowns are lifted. America now stands in contrast to some other parts of the world, such as the euro area, where markets expect a prolonged disinflationary slump (see Finance section).

    • beef up transitive verb. [beef up sth, beef sth up] 增加‹resources, budget, control›
  • grant |BrE: ɡrɑːnt, AmE: ɡrænt| noun. (award of money) 拨款
  • underpin |BrE: ʌndəˈpɪn, AmE: ˌəndərˈpɪn| transitive verb.

    • (strengthen) «fact, statement» 支持‹argument, case›; «morality» 巩固‹society›
  • lift |BrE: lɪft, AmE: lɪft| transitive verb.
    • (end) 撤销‹ban, sanctions, curfew›; 解除‹embargo, siege›
    • (remove) 去除. this reprieve lifts a great deal of the pressure off him
      这份缓刑令让他感觉轻松了不少
  • slump |BrE: slʌmp, AmE: sləmp| noun.
    • (fall in value, profit etc.) 骤降; a slump in the property market 房地产市场的急剧衰退
    • (period of low economic activity) 萧条期.the company is slowly recovering from a prolonged slump 该公司正从漫长的低迷中慢慢恢复过来
  • up front adverb.
    • (towards the front) 在前面 ‹pass, go›;
    • (in advance) 预先 ‹pay, put›. the deal is going nowhere until we’ve got the money up front 在我们弄到钱之前这项生意不会有进展

  • Federal Reserve noun. US 联邦储备银行

But the bill also brings dangers. Markets cheered it not just because of its likely effect on growth, but also because it directly benefits investors. Large firms will have access to cheap taxpayer-financed loans on an unprecedented scale. The Fed had already announced, on March 23rd, that it would buy companies’ short-term debt. Congress is now giving the central bank a capital infusion to support vast direct lending to corporate America. Emergency interventions like these shift the costs of the crisis away from investors and towards taxpayers (see Free exchange).

  • infusion |BrE: ɪnˈfjuːʒ(ə)n, AmE: ɪnˈfjuʒən| noun. uncountable and countable (of element, quality) 注入
    • an infusion of sth into sb/sth; 将某观念向某人的灌输/将某资源向某处的注入
    • an infusion of new capital into a company. 对公司的新资金注入
  • corporate |BrE: ˈkɔːp(ə)rət, AmE: ˈkɔrp(ə)rət| adjective.
    • (collective) 全体的 ‹action, decision›; 共同的 ‹ownership, responsibility›
    • Business,Finance 公司的 ‹policy, assets, profits›
    • (united) 联合的 ‹body, institution›
  • emergency interventions 紧急情况干预
  • shift A away from B and towards C 把A从B转移到C

That is partly necessary, because letting a large number of big firms go bankrupt could prove so disruptive that it would worsen the crisis. And investors are not completely undeserving beneficiaries. No firm could have been expected to stockpile to pay workers to do nothing in the event of a government-enforced lockdown. The real potential unfairness in the stimulus is that aid to large firms is the part of the rescue package that is most likely to work without a hitch. Sophisticated corporations will have no trouble borrowing from the Fed or from backstopped capital markets, even as they lay off some workers (prohibited only “to the extent practicable”). But aid to small firms, and directly to the jobless, may turn out to be less than comprehensive.

  • undeserving | BrE: ʌndɪˈzəːvɪŋ, AmE: ˌəndəˈzərvɪŋ| adjective. (not worthy of help) 不该得到的; (not worthy of praise or reward) 不配的

Small businesses employ 52% of private-sector workers. Half have a cash buffer of less than one month, by one estimate. The bill starts a new programme to lend to them directly. The part of any government loan used to pay wages, utility costs, rent or mortgages will be forgiven—except if firms lay off workers, in which case the subsidy will be reduced in proportion to the number of jobs lost. This scheme is more complex than those in some European countries, under which governments are paying most of the wages of suspended workers. It is hard to imagine that its administration will be quick and efficient. And some analysts think the pot of money on offer is too small (see United States section). Those most adept at navigating the bureaucracy may get most of the benefit.

    • private-sector 私营部分
  • buffer |BrE: ˈbʌfə, AmE: ˈbəfər| noun. figurative (protection) (thing) 缓冲物; (person) 缓冲人. gold is the best buffer against inflation
    黄金是抵御通货膨胀的最有效屏障

  • pot informal (large sum of money) 巨款. to have/make pots of money 有了/挣了一大笔钱; (pooled funds) the pot 凑集的资金
    • the pot of money 凑集的钱

  • suspended worker = laid-off worker 被解雇/暂时停职的工人

Cash handouts to the public are simple enough. But $1,200 is not much help for a laid-off worker. The jobless will rely on the bill’s temporary expansion of unemployment insurance benefits by $600 per week. Yet America cannot construct an adequate social safety-net overnight. For example, 8.5% of Americans lack health insurance. Some workers who are laid off will join their ranks just as they face a heightened risk of falling ill.

  • handout |BrE: ˈhandaʊt, AmE: ˈhændˌaʊt| noun. (payment) 捐款 ; (gift) 捐赠物
    • to live off/rely on handouts 靠别人的施舍生活
  • heightened risk 提高的风险
  • safety-net figurative (safeguard) 安全措施
  • join the rank 加入行列

Make no mistake: we would vote for the bill, most of which is urgent and necessary. But it is far from perfect. And just like past emergency stimulus, it could work better for big firms than for anyone else, leaving a lingering sense of injustice.

    • (slow) 拖延的 a painful, lingering death 痛苦而缓慢的死亡
