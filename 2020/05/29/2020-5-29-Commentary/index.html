How to comment on a philosophy text?

Classnotes and homework of Methodology of Philosophy(2020 Spring Term): How to comment on a text?

Class notes

Commenting means to propose a philosophical interpretation of the text.

Discription

Describing the content/structure of the short passage we comment on.Before commenting, we should first describe, shortly and accurately, what the text says. To make this description different from paraphrasis, we need to focus on the structure and meaning of the short passage we are commenting on.

Example:

“The sun is not sight, but isn’t it the cause of sight itself and isn’t it seen by it?”

“That’s right.”

“Let’s say, then, that the sun is what I called the offspring of the good, which the good begot as its analogue. What the good itself is in the intelligible realm, in relation to understanding and intelligible things, the sun is in the visible realm, in relation to sight and visible things.”

Do not write: “First Socrates states the difference between the sun and sight: sun is the cause of sight and is seen by it. Then, reminding that the sun is begotten by the good, he sketches the analogy between the sun in the visible world and the good in the intelligible world.”

Write instead: “The analogy between the sun and the good is proposed on the basis of two triple distinctions: the causes of sight and of knowledge (the sun and the good, respectively), the object of sight or knowledge (visible things and, as we learn later, the forms), and the part of ourselves that sees or knows (the eye, and as we later, the soul). These levels are connected together by processes: sight (for the eye) and understanding (for the soul).”

In the latter example, the description of the content of the text never simply repeats the text, but instead takes some distance from the order in which the text presents the information, to better synthetize the philosophical meaning of these few lines.

Commenting on a short passage

Commenting means making it clear what is the philosophical meaning of the text, by explaining how the meaning unfolds in the text, and its implications. In general, you can comment on several different levels: the words, the grammar/ syntax, and the argument.

  • Commenting on a word means explaining or defining a concept. E.g., defining “Intelligible realm.” You can also use etymology if you know it.

  • Commenting on grammar/syntax means to explain how the text suggests effects of meaning that are philosophically relevant, by means of the arrangement of ideas in the sentence.

In this extract, you could for instance comment on the parallelism in grammar of:

“What the good itself is in the intelligible realm, in relation to understanding and intelligible things, the sun is in the visible realm, in relation to sight and visible things.”

Example of commentary on this sentence: “The very structure of this sentence elaborates on the parallel between the depiction of the visible world and that of the intelligible world: by means of grammar, Plato clearly suggests not only that the good and the sun play the same role in their respective world, but also that the structure of the relationships in which they are involved, in their respective world, with the objects of sight and knowledge are the same. The analogy is indeed by definition not a comparison between elements of a relationship, but a comparison of relationships.”

Commenting on the structure of the argument: this means drawing a hierarchy between the elements of an arguments, as elaborated on the text. Concretely, you should make it clear what is the status of each element of an argument in the whole argument. For instance, mayebe the first couple of sentences in a text express the problem that the author is addressing; the next sentence the solution he is proposing; then, the reason why this is a convinving solution; finally, examples.

In the short passage under study here, you could for instance stress that the organization of the cues of Socrates shows that the most fundamental idea, which is expressed first, is that the sun and good are the causes and potential obejcts of sight and knowledge. That, after Glaucon’s approval of this idea, Plato explains the details of the analogy, but that he adds relevant dimensions progressively, when they are needed for his argument (the idea that sight cannot see by night, for instance, is added later in the text).

Techniques to comment on a short passage:

In general, the commentary can rely on several techniques:

  • Find examples to make your understanding of the text clearer to your reader.
  • Draw sketchs, compose tables. Note however that a sketch or a table needs itself to be commented on in a sentence or two, to make your point clear.
  • Provide definition, use etymology.
  • Comment on the grammar, on the syntax.

Example of commentary

Text:

“The sun is not sight, but isn’t it the cause of sight itself and isn’t it seen by it?”

“That’s right.”

“Let’s say, then, that the sun is what I called the offspring of the good, which the good begot as its analogue. What the good itself is in the intelligible realm, in relation to understanding and intelligible things, the sun is in the visible realm, in relation to sight and visible things.”

Commentary:

The analogy between the sun and the good is proposed on the basis of two triple distinctions: the causes of sight and of knowledge (the sun and the good, respectively), the object of sight or knowledge (visible things and, as we learn later, the forms), and the part of ourselves that sees or knows (the eye, and as we later, the soul). These levels are connected together by processes: sight (for the eye) and understanding (for the soul).

The organization of the cues of Socrates shows that the most fundamental idea, which is expressed first, is that the sun and good are the causes and potential objects of sight and knowledge. This idea is the most fundamental one also for the whole text, insofar as it gives the direction to the whole analogy, and also prepares the identity of degree between ontology and epistemology, which Socrates phrases later in the text.

After Glaucon’s approval of this view, Plato explains the details of the analogy. He will add relevant dimensions progressively, when they are needed for his argument (the idea that sight cannot see by night, for instance, is added later in the text).

The first sentence in this passage stresses the analogical relationship between the sun and the good. By “offspring of the good,” Socrates refers to a notion that was introduced just before our passage: in Plato’s theory of the intelligible and perceptible worlds, the latter is created by the former. The “good” thus refers to the form of the good.

The very structure of the last sentence, where the details of the analogy are explained, elaborates on the parallel between the depiction of the visible world and that of the intelligible world:

“What the good itself is in the intelligible realm, in relation to understanding and intelligible things, the sun is in the visible realm, in relation to sight and visible things.”

By means of grammar, Plato clearly suggests not only that the good and the sun play the same role in their respective world, but also that the structure of the relationships in which they are involved, in their respective world, with the objects of sight and knowledge are the same. The analogy is indeed by definition not a comparison between elements of a relationship, but a comparison of relationships, as we could represent in this sketch:

As an analogy is a comparison of relationships, the point of Plato’s comparison here is to show the necessity to suppose a form that makes knowledge (or: understanding) of other forms possible. The analogy has not only a descriptive function, but also a persuasive role in the argument: it is obvious that light is necessary to sight, but it is less clear that the good is necesary to knowledge of the others forms. The analogy is, here, dissymmetrical, because Plato suggests that this similitude in relationships between the two worlds is caused by the fact that the good begot the sun; in this sense, the sensible world is a reflection of the intelligible world.

Homework: A commentary on Plato, Republic, VII, 517d–518d.

Text:

– But our present discussion, on the other hand, shows that the power to learn is present in everyone’s soul and that the instrument with which each learns is like an eye that cannot be turned around from darkness to light without turning the whole body. This instrument cannot be turned around from that which is coming into being without turning the whole soul until it is able to study that which is and the brightest thing that is, namely, the one we call the good. Isn’t that right?

– Yes.

– Then education is the craft concerned with doing this very thing, this turning around, and with how the soul can most easily and effectively be made to do it. It isn’t the craft of putting sight into the soul. Education takes for granted that sight is there but that it isn’t turned the right way or looking where it ought to look, and it tries to redirect it appropriately.

Socrates tries to illustrate the effect of education through the analogy between sun and the fire in a cavelike dwelling, as we could represent in this sketch:

Education doesn’t provide knowledge to the soul like putting sight into the blind, but a craft directing the right way to the good. Everyone has the potential to learn, but they are always obsessed with the evil of human life. With the help of education, they can look at divine things in a just way.

The first sentence disputes the declaration that education puts new knowledge into souls due to an understatement of education’s function. With an analogy between knowledge and sight, Socrates tells us education is an instrument to turn the whole soul into the direction to face the source of light - the good, not just provides sight to eyes, which only involved part of the whole body.

After Glaucon’s agreement with this claim, Socrates stresses the function of education as a craft to turn us in an appropriate way while we have already know how to use our eyes but not in a right way. A right way to look around means we can learn the knowledge with a most easy and effective move - turning around - namely, education.

Craft(technê): Plato develops a complex account of technê. First of all, a craft has a function (ergon);

Through the metaphor of fettered human living in a cave, Socrates hints we are always trapped in the reflection of the real world without realizing what’s literally happening in the actual world. Education doesn’t mean there’s a prophet describing the landscape of the real word to us, but demonstrates us how to achieve the real world and explore it by our own eyes.

The shadow of real world also signifies a limitation of the method we are born with to obtain the knowledge while there’s a wilder intelligible wolrd we cannot experience due to the less effective way we look at it. Education provides us a way directly - just as easily as turning around, which is not only a effective method but also in a best sense.

The essential point of education is not about the range of knowledge, but the awareness of the brightest thing - the good. The shadow projected on the screen can be classified as knowledge, but with a direction aganist the fire, as an analogy of the good. Without an appropriate goal to chase, we can never understand the whole world we are living in.

Socrate clarifies the function of education as a craft to lead the whole soul to the good with the comparison between putting sight into blind eyes and turning the object of sight from projected shadow of the real world to the world itself. The goal of education is to explore the intelligible world, not pour the static knowledge of intelligible world into limited souls.
