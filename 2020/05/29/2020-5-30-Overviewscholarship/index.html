An overview of scholarship of the problem ‘what is the unhypothetical principle in Plato’s Republic?‘

Guideline

  • Views according to which the good is the unhypothetical principle. [Adam 1902; Mason]
    • It means that the form and/or its instanciations are good.
      • Individual forms (justice, beauty…) are good
      • Instanciations of the forms (a just soul, a beautiful city) are good
      • Both instanciations of the forms and individual forms are good.

Homework (Baltzly part)

One suggestion is that an unhypothetical starting point is a proposition that is established by showing that its denial is self-refuting in a certain sense (Baltzly 1996 1 , 1999;. criticized in Bailey 2006).

  1. The first strategy that identifies dialectic’s unhypothetical first principle with the good is ontological and equates the unhypothetical aspect of the arche in dialectic with the ontological supremacy of the form of the good (R. 509b6-10).
  2. The second strategy is epistemological and treats the unhypothetical character of the dialectician’s arche as a matter of the kind of argument used to establish it.

According to Baltzly, we obtain two different interpretations of the unhypothetical first principle form perspectives of ontology and epistemology, respectively.

If we consider the unhypothetical first principle as the form of the good, we have to assume that dialetic’s end point is also its unhypothetical starting point because Plato compares dialectic to a journey towards the grasp of the good itself. (R.532a-b) Tracing back to the Republic, dialectic treats hypotheses as stepping stones to ascend to an unhypothetical starting point of everything, and grasps this principle to come down to a conclusion, which implies that dialectic’s end point is a “conclusion”. Apparently, this sketch is not compatible with the content of the good, which includes justice and beauty instead of tons of conclusions. Maybe Baltzly makes a mistake here for considering the journey of dialectic towards the good as the whole process of dialectic, while Plato refers to the first phase of dialectic from hypotheses to the first principle of everything. Therefore, the limitations of assuming the first principle as the good doesn’t exit, and this ontology interpretation is reasonable.

If we consider the unhypothetical first principle as a method setting a foundation for dialectic to turn the unquestioned hypotheses of mathematics into knowledge (R. 533b6-c5), the unhypothetical first principle is an argument that we cannot reject unless we refute ourselves. (Parmenides. 143b-4a) But this statement supposes an epistemological use of unhypothetical first principle globally, while Plato puts it in the middle of the Republic.

Apart from the position of this statement in the whole book, assuming the first principle as a method eliminates the significance and specificity of this principle as unhypothetical and involving everything. We have lots of methods to turn unquestioned hypotheses into knowledge, but dialectic is important because the first phase of it is grasping the good, namely, the brightest thing as a sun. We can consider the unhypothetical first principle as a method, but we cannot discard the property dialectic involved, which is the role of the good.
